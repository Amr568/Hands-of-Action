Population

Population plays a central role in determining the course of global progress and sustainability, as a path towards a more sustainable future requires demographic foresight. With over 8 billion people on the planet as of 2023, the interplay between population growth, resources, and sustainable development has never been more crucial.

How is it related to sustainable development?

Population growth and its management are foundational to achieving the SDGs. Each of the 17 SDGs is influenced by population dynamics, as they determine demand for essential services, resource consumption, and environmental sustainability.

Rapid population growth is closely linked to hunger, food insecurity, and poverty, as it puts pressure on resources and the economy. For instance, high dependency ratios caused by rapid population growth challenge sustainable economic growth. It also adds challenges when it comes to education, since ensuring access to a quality education in countries with growing cohorts of children and youth presents a significant challenge. On the health side, maternal, neonatal, and nutritional conditions continue to be among the most common causes of death in countries with rapid population growth. High population density contributes to the spread of communicable diseases and puts a burden on health systems. In such countries, gender inequality is expressed in terms of fewer girls than boys going to school, high rates of girls giving birth before reaching adulthood, and fewer women than men in formal employment or political life.

Why is it challenging?

Extreme population growth rates in many countries (high or low) endanger their social and economic stability. While some countries, like those in sub-Saharan Africa, have high population growth rates, other countries, like East Asian countries and some European countries, face population decline. Unbalanced population growth rates drive many challenges locally and globally; they include:

  • Youth Unemployment

    Countries with youthful populations, such as those in sub-Saharan Africa, face high unemployment rates due to insufficient education and job opportunities that don't cover the wide population.

  • Rapid Growth and Urbanization

    Urbanization has risen significantly, with over 56% of the global population now living in cities. While urbanization offers economic opportunities, in many developing nations, it has also led to overcrowding, inadequate infrastructure, and environmental degradation.

  • Aging Populations

    Developed countries, like those in Europe and East Asia, are also facing population-related challenges such as declining fertility and aging populations. For instance, Japan has over 28% of its population aged 65 or older. This old population forms a burden on the economy and health systems in Japan and other countries with aging populations.

  • Migration

    Migration reshapes demographics globally and cannot be ignored as a reason for high population density. High migration rates may reduce labor shortages in some developed nations but increase social integration challenges. Conversely, emigration in developing countries leads to economic and skill loss.

What about Egypt?

Egypt's population exceeded 105 million in 2024, with over 50% of Egyptians under 25, making it the most populous Arab country.

The youthful population of Egypt represents both a challenge and an opportunity. While it could drive economic growth, it raises the dependency ratio and pressures education, health, and employment systems. Additionally, with nearly 43% of the population living in urban areas, cities like Cairo and Alexandria are overcrowded and face internal transportation problems. Also, agricultural lands face the danger of being abandoned due to internal migration from the countryside to cities. This can bring a shortage in food resources with the current agricultural system in Egypt.

How you can volunteer to help.

You can help against many of the population-related challenges by volunteering in these fields:

  • Spreading Awareness

    Volunteers can drive awareness campaigns on family planning and reproductive health in underserved communities.

  • Health initiatives

    Volunteering initiatives provide essential healthcare services in remote areas, reducing maternal and child mortality rates. These initiatives also involve training community health workers in regions with limited healthcare infrastructure.

  • Promoting Sustainability

    Environmental volunteers play a vital role in educating communities about sustainable practices, such as efficient water usage and waste management. These efforts help reduce the environmental impact of population growth.

  • Empowering Youth

    Programs like the UN Volunteers (UNV) initiative engage youth in development projects, equipping them with skills while addressing community challenges.
